The orbit

Last updated on January 27, 2022

created: 2021-05-18 09:18

I’ve been postponing writing at length about this, because it’s kind of a key ingredient and deserves, nay commands, my utmost attention, but for now, I’ll just mention a couple of key things/notes…

- I don’t want to call it the microcosmic orbit. I’m not sure why, but I just don’t. Something seems off and a little manufacture by reusing that term that you see in almost every other text about Tao or Chi Kung. I might just call it the orbit, but somehow that doesn’t quite fit, at least not in my head.

- I think I’ve already covered this, but to reiterate, the orbit is best done after sexual yoga, what we will benignly call “generation” yoga, not to be confused with Naropa’s generation stage yoga. This is the development of the mind-body state inducive of having sex, so the envisioning of a sexual union with a consort, kissing, and with the four chakras (navel, heart, throat, and crown of head) also touching and kissing. The bases of these chakras are situated within a vaccuus column. This column is about the thicknes of a straw give or take. One first expands this column to encompass the universe all the while feeling empty and vacuous, and then shrunk to smaller than a thread yet still vacuous, then settling to the size of about a common sipping straw. Once that visualization is seen/felt, one begins visualizing the union. The generation is more vivid and longer for seated meditation

- the breath. Some say breathing exercises should happen before and/or after the orbit, some say physical exercises. Many texts disagree on how, when, what, or why these should be performed. Some say hold your breath, some say you should absolutely never do that. I will just lay out below what works for me

- the actual orbit is simple but seem key points are noted below. To start, for most people it has been said the orbit should take two breaths. Where these breaths happen during the orbit is up to the practitioner, but some texts lay down rules of how it should be done, and, as it turns, disagree with each other, and even sometimes with themselves. So, again, you need to figure out what works best for you, and it may depend some on your physical condition

- Here’s how I do it. It’s good to think of the place just below the navel and in about a couple inches (kind of the center of the lower belly) as a storage spot for “energy”; the hara, or dan ‘tien’. I put energy in quotes because even though this word is overused, I can’t really think of a better one, and “essence” sounds just a little cheesy and is sometimes confused with the spirit, which would further confuse things for people following chi kung - spirit is different than chi. Breathe in to that spot. The breath is deeper than the diaphragm and not chest should be involved at all. This is deep belly breathing, and it should be very relaxed. The posture should be as erect as can be (straight spine) without discomfort. An effort should be made to have the skeleton support the posture and not muscles. During the first exhale the energy gently follows a route that descends into the genetils, glides toward the anus across the perineum and enters the central channel (straw) right in front of the coccyx (tail bone), goes up until it hits the first chakra of note, the one in the channel just behind where it started. It does not pause there and the breath seamlessly moves to an inhalation (see breath. This seamlessness is important. There should be no holding of breath and the energy should not stop moving. It continues up with deep inhalation (not sharp intake, relaxed), through the heart chakra, and to the throat where the exhalation starts. It continues up to the crown and gently drops over the forehead passing by the “third eye” between the eyebrows to the tip of the tongue (which should be always lightly placed right behind the front teeth at the roof of the mouth) and flows down the throat during the inhaltion, through the internal organs. The second inhalation completes as the energy has hit where it started ready to keep moving. So, that i basically it in a nutshell.

key points

- it is importan to maintain no mind, if the mind wanders, that is okay, just bring it back to thinking of nothing. If it keeps wandering, don’t worry, with practice this will become less of a problem. Sometimes your mind doesn’t want to cooperate. It’s not big deal, not the end of the world. Just keep going.

- fear may set in. This is perfectly normal, and is actually a good thing, because it means you are making progress, a little doubt is actually reassurance. It goes away. Just remember that little voice that says, “everything is going to be okay”. This is an important thing to embrace when bootstrapping with no external support or help - there may be overexcitement at first. This is okay too

- there may be sexual excitement, even orgasm. This is fine. Just see it for what it is, and don’t expect it, which brings me to…

- don’t have any expectations. This will only lead to frustration. Do not judge yourself, grade yourself, or make a performance report. This is not like training for a sport. This benefits are subtle, nuanced, and not obvious. There is no objective scorecard

- sometimes it is good to visualize the energy as it physically being something, like a warm marble for example. In inner heat yoga, the energy is more sublime and takes the form a of a flame. Sometimes it is useful to think of it as a massage, meaning something that is happening that you are allowing to happen, and not forcing. In other words, keep a leash on your will. No furrowed brows allowed, which brings me to…

- soft eyes. This is huge. It is so important, I probably should have mentioned it first. Soft eyes doesn’t just mean relaxed, it means sublime like the eyes of someone who has fallen in love.

- if you start to salivate excessively, that’s okay, that’s normal and healthy

- if you get an erection or, if female, moist, that’s okay, nothing weird

- if you sweat, still okay

- if you get a headache, not okay, it’s time to back off and do some empty mind, which is what you should conclude with anyway

- concluding is best done with full no mind (what is no mind)

- in tantric Tibetan yoga, it is customary to arise from meditation and carry the no-mind bliss from that experience into the real world. This may be hard for the lay person to effectively do throughout the entire day. It sould be sufficient to just be mindful of the changed perception of reality for a few moments into regular dayly life, seeing one’s surrounding as a manifestation of one’s mandala of bright colors and sharp contrast, and all sentient beings as living enlightened beings

- there is a possibility that you inadvertently tensed/jerked some muscles/tendons during meditation. A good rotating stretch will loosen things up and get the blood flowing. Best done sitting. Spinning the torso from the base of the spine up is recommended by Tsonkhapa. He gives specifics on the exercise. I think it’s fine for anyone to come up with their own version of stretching the muscles around the spine.
